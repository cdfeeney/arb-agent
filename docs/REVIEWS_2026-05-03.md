## EXIT LOGIC: YES — capital velocity dominates

The reviewer's claim — "you're leaving 60% on the table by exiting at
40-50% capture; just hold to resolution" — is the textbook beginner
mistake of optimizing absolute return per trade instead of return per
dollar-day. It is wrong by an order of magnitude.

**The arithmetic.** Take the empirical Kashkari pair: predicted
$0.56/contract, realized $0.28 in roughly 1 day. The reviewer wants the
remaining $0.28 over the ~30 days to resolution. Annualized: early exit
is $0.28 × 365 = $102/contract-year; hold-to-resolution adds $0.28/30 ×
365 = $3.40/contract-year on the *same locked capital*. The early exit
is a **30× higher** annualized rate, and the freed capital can re-enter
the next 4%-edge opportunity tomorrow. Across 7 trades, $1.24 realized
in days vs. waiting weeks for $3.07 is not "60% left on the table" —
it's a Sharpe-dominant strategy because cycle time collapses.

**Why no absolute `sum_bids > 1.0` floor.** Such a gate is equivalent to
demanding *all* convergence before exiting. It pretends the convergence
option has zero time value, which contradicts the entire reason we
entered: edges decay, books thin, counterparties resolve adversarially.
`position_monitor.py:307-314` states this explicitly: "with average
per-trade returns of ~10%, capturing 40-50% of an arb in hours and
redeploying beats holding 30 days for 100%, by ~30-100× annualized."
That is the math, not a vibe.

**The reviewer's risk concern is already priced in.** Two layered gates
handle exactly the failure mode an absolute floor would:

1. `min_capture_above_fees = 1.5×` blocks the trade-#379 class of bug
   where gross $0.153 barely cleared $0.124 in exit fees — old gate
   fired, hold would have netted $0.21 more.
2. `annualized_multiple ≥ 1.5×` requires that exiting *now* annualizes
   to ≥1.5× holding-to-close, the correct opportunity-cost frame.
3. `_try_resolution_spike_capture` enforces
   `near_resolution_spike_fee_multiple` inside the force-hold window so
   we don't bleed the spread on dead-weight time.

**Real-options framing.** Holding to resolution is American-style early
exercise. When the underlying convergence has already moved 40-50% in
1 day with no remaining drift expected (most edge-decay is front-loaded
post-entry), early exercise is optimal — the remaining time-value is
dominated by carry, slippage, and counterparty redeploy opportunity.

### Summary

- Capital velocity: $0.28 in 1 day annualizes to ~30× holding $0.56
  over 30 days.
- An absolute `sum_bids > 1.0` gate ignores opportunity cost and kills
  compounding on a repeatable 4%-edge book.
- The 1.5× exit-fee buffer + 1.5× annualized-multiple +
  resolution-spike override already neutralize the fee-leak risk that
  motivates the floor.

## EXIT LOGIC: NO — capital velocity is a fairy tale

**To: $1,000-deployer. From: Risk. Verdict: STAND DOWN.**

The author yanked the `sum_bids > 1.0 + exit_fee/c` floor in 2eff1a8
with the gloss "capital velocity wins." It does not. It cannot, on this
evidence base. The argument is built on three lies and a sample size of
three.

**Lie #1: The annualized_multiple gate is a "right frame."** Look at
line 1048: `annualized_now_pct = (mtm/cost_basis) * (365/days_held)`.
With `days_held=0.083` (the test value, ~2 hours), any positive net is
annualized at 11,000%+. Compared against `annualized_to_close` for a
30-day arb (~12×), the gate fires on literally any cent of net. That
is not a discipline; it is a microscope amplifying noise into signal.
Worse, this math assumes redeployment occurs continuously — your
observed clearing rate is **zero opportunities at 4%**. Idle capital
between trades is unmodeled. The "30-100×" annualized lift in the
comment block at line 311 is a phantom: it divides by `days_held`
while pretending `days_idle` is also `days_held`.

**Lie #2: `min_capture_above_fees=1.5` is a safety floor.** It is a
buffer against fee-math bugs — that's literally what the line 286
comment says ("the broken-fee-math class of bug"). It does NOT protect
against exiting at $0.92 sum_bids when holding to $1.00 was worth
another $0.08/contract minus zero exit fees. You traded a known $0.08
of locked profit for a 1.5× exit-fee buffer of maybe $0.04 net. That's
a negative-expectancy swap on every PARTIAL_UNWIND below par.

**Lie #3: "10% per trade × redeploy beats hold-to-resolution."**
Empirical reality: 7 closed trades, $1.24 realized vs $3.07 predicted
= **40% capture**. Five of seven are the same Kashkari pair —
N=3 independent. The 50% predicted-vs-realized gap on the only
repeating pair IS the convergence-reversal and thin-book risk you've
now removed the gate against. No measured Sharpe, no drawdown
distribution, no time-to-redeploy distribution.

**Structural holes the gate-removal exposes:**

- `_decide` sizes off `best_bid_size` (line 253) — tests use 10c. At
  $1k × 50c, you walk the book, slippage gate (line 271, 2%) fires
  WATCH, capital sits.
- Maker-exit rests at `best_bid + 0.01` for 24h (`d04b541`). If poly
  bid drifts down 2¢ post-rest, you're an adverse-selection magnet at
  the worst price in the book, hedge half-broken.
- Convergence reversal: partial-unwind at 0.97 leaves residual
  contracts. If sum_bids drops back to 0.92, residual is now
  underwater AND you've burned exit fees.

### Summary

- Capital-velocity math is survivorship bias on `days_held`; idle time
  and redeploy rate are unmeasured — current real rate ≈ 0.
- N=3 independent samples + 40% capture rate ≠ ready for $1,000 live
  deployment; rebuild evidence to N≥30 with the floor restored.
- Removing the absolute hold-vs-exit gate trades known locked profit
  ($1 - sum_bids) for a fee buffer that doesn't price
  convergence-reversal or thin-book adverse selection.
